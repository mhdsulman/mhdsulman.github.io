Course Description:  This is a graduate level course designed as a core course for the interdisciplinary applied science and mathematics (IAMS) PhD program. It provides both mathematical analysis, numerical algorithms and programming concepts for solving practical problems in life and physical sciences as well as applied mathematics.

 

Syllabus:

·      Floating point arithmetic, conditioning and stability

·      Linear systems of equations (direct and iterative methods)

·      Eigenvalue problems and singular value decomposition (SVD)

·      Numerical Interpolation

·      Numerical differentiation and integration methods

·      Nonlinear equations and unconstraint optimization

·      Ordinary and partial differential equations

·      Fast Fourier transforms         

 

Prerequisites: Multivariable calculus (e.g. MTH 2320 or equivalent), differential equations (e.g. MTH2330, MTH 2350 or equivalent) and linear algebra (e.g. MTH 2530 or equivalent). Some Basic knowledge of partial differential equations is expected. Familiarity with writing computer programs (in Matlab, Python, C++, C, Fortran or any other language) is assumed. Matlab will be the main language used for the course, please review Matlab before the first class; a good place to start is Mathworks Matlab documentation “Getting Started” , Wright State University now offers all students a free copy of Matlab to install on their personal computers, please check CATS website.

Textbooks: The lecture notes will mostly be extracted from

·       Scientific Computing with Matlab and Octave, by Alfio Quarteroni and Fausto Saleri, 4th Ed.

·       Data-Driven Modeling and Scientific Computing, by J. Nathan Kutz, Oxford University Press, 2013.

·       Scientific Computing with Case Studies, by Diane O’Leary, SIAM 2009.

Matlab References:

·       Numerical Computing with MATLAB, by Cleve Moler

·       Computational guidelines, University of Cambridge, Department of Engineering.

·       Matlab An Introduction with Applications by Amos Gilat, 4th Ed.
